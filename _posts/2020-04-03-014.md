##### [The cold and empty balcony of a fancy Manhattan mansion, the remains of a party still ongoing just inside the doors]

##### [JOHN FOGG leans against the balcony railing, nursing a scotch on the rocks]

#### JACK 

_[singing to himself, a little drunk]_ Should auld acquaintance be forgot, And never brought to mind? Should auld acquaintance be forgot, And the days of auld lang– 

##### [The balcony door opens, letting in the sound of the ruckus inside, before it’s closed again, CHARLES CHAMBERS coming onto the balcony, muttering to himself]

#### CHARLIE  

Godforsaken nosy good-for-nothing -

#### JACK

Well, that’s certainly a way to be greeted. 

#### CHARLIE  

Oh god! _[then, settling]_ Oh my goodness, Mister Fogg. 

#### JACK 

Mr. Chambers. 

#### CHARLIE 

I didn’t realize anyone was out here. 

#### JACK 

Not anyone. Me. 

#### CHARLIE 

Yes, well, I didn’t realize that either. 

#### JACK 

If you’d like me to vacate, I’m afraid you’re out of luck. An empty balcony is valuable real estate at a party like this. 

#### CHARLIE  

Not empty anymore. 

#### JACK 

No. 

#### CHARLIE 

Right, so I’ll just… _[then]_ Are you- are you hiding from someone? Only it’s not like you to remove yourself from the festivities. 

#### JACK 

How on earth would you know, we haven’t been at the same party for...ten years?

##### [Charlie walks to the railing, coming to stand next to Jack]

#### CHARLIE 

Has it really been ten years? 

#### JACK  

Nearly. It would’ve been the fair, wouldn’t it? 

#### CHARLIE 

Goodness, I suppose that’s true. Longer even, since we were both here. 

#### JACK

That’s right. What was it...eighty-eight? Eighty-nine? 

#### CHARLIE 

I believe it was technically ninety by the time we actually spoke. New Year’s Day, eighteen-ninety. 

#### JACK 

There’s that steel trap memory of yours. My recollection is clouded by the hangover to end all hangovers. 

#### CHARLIE  

That’s precisely why I remember the year so exactly. I don’t think I’ve ever started a year in such rough shape. It’s half the reason I haven’t come to one of Hayward’s New Year’s shindigs since. 

#### JACK 

What brings you to this one? 

#### CHARLIE 

I’m only just arrived back in New York. What better way to get reacquainted with society than a party that everyone is at? 

#### JACK 

You’re not going to be doing much reacquainting out here with me. 

#### CHARLIE 

I needed some fresh air. 

#### JACK 

No snow storm to keep you from getting a cab this time. You could get fresh air at your own home. 

#### CHARLIE 

Recollection not so clouded after all, it seems. _[beat]_ I’m staying at Astor House at the moment, as it happens. Hardly home. 

#### JACK 

Leagues better, I’d imagine. Where on earth have you been the last ten years to afford staying there? 

#### CHARLIE  

Europe, mostly. 

#### JACK 

Touring?

#### CHARLIE

Some lecturing as well. 

#### JACK

Lecturing? What on earth about? 

#### CHARLIE  

The finer details of illusion and technology. And, increasingly, spiritualism. It whipped up quite the frenzy there. 

#### JACK 

Here too. I find the whole business fairly fascinating but I cannot fathom what you would have to say about it. 

#### CHARLIE  

The history and philosophy of it is quite intertwined with the history of magic. It’s not such a far leap. 

#### JACK 

It is for a man as incredulous as you. 

#### CHARLIE 

I don’t need to believe in something to speak on it. 

#### JACK  

Nor practice it. 

#### CHARLIE 

Oh, Mister Fogg, will we never stop having this disagreement? The magic we do and the magic of psychics and mediums are animals of a different stripe. Our audiences know that they’re being tricked, that’s the point. 

#### JACK 

What if it isn’t a trick? 

#### CHARLIE

What do you mean? 

##### [Beat. Jack takes a sip]

#### JACK  

Forget it. _[then]_ I’m- well, it’s one in the morning on New Year’s Eve, I’m drunk. 

#### CHARLIE 

New Year’s Day. Technically. 

#### JACK 

_[scoffs]_ 

#### CHARLIE  

I never took you for a maudlin drunk. 

#### JACK 

Excuse me? 

#### CHARLIE 

I would’ve expected you to become more jovial the more you imbibe, not be inclined to get the morbs.

#### JACK 

I do become more jovial. Usually. New Year’s Eve is just a particularly beloathed holiday. 

#### CHARLIE

Really? Why? 

##### [Jack straightens up and turns to Charlie, more aggressive]

#### JACK 

How have you been the past ten years, Mister Chambers? Off in Europe, garnering immense success it seems, but how have you been faring? Physically, psychologically? 

#### CHARLIE 

What kind of question is that? 

#### JACK 

In the last decade of galavanting on the continent raking in the dollars, did you once stop to think about how my life was going here in America? 

#### CHARLIE 

Mister Fogg 

#### JACK  
I think I’m safe to assume that the answer to that is a resounding no. Which is perplexing, and more than a little insulting, considering you obliterated my career and then left on the next ship out of New York harbor. 

#### CHARLIE 

I beg your pardon? 

#### JACK 

Don’t behave as if this surprises you. I imagine this is the exact conversation you’ve been running from for ten years. 

#### CHARLIE 

I truly do not know what you’re speaking of. I’ve been in Europe because it was about time I did Europe. 

#### JACK 

Because you’d already successfully toured all through North America. 

#### CHARLIE 

That was certainly part of it. 

#### JACK 

And what was the rest of it? Fleeing the scene of the crime, perhaps? 

#### CHARLIE 

The scene of the- Fogg, what are you talking about? 

#### JACK 

The World’s Fair, Chambers, that’s what. 

#### CHARLIE 

Don’t you go blaming me for your sloppy showmanship -

#### JACK 

Sloppy -

#### CHARLIE 

You would not have been humiliated the way you were if you had simply listened -

#### JACK 

Humiliated, my god, that’s a strong way of putting it. 

#### CHARLIE

Is that not exactly your complaint? 

#### JACK  

My complaint is that you sabotaged my illusion and made me look a fool in front of all of Chicago. 

#### CHARLIE  

Sabotaged? Is that what you’ve been thinking all this time, that I sabotaged you? 

#### JACK 

How else would you account for the act failing? 

#### CHARLIE 

The act failed because it was a foolish, risky, and entirely absurd notion to begin with -

#### JACK 

_[overlapping]_ An absurd notion we carried off quite successfully for -

#### CHARLIE 

I didn’t sabotage you, what would I have to gain? 

#### JACK 

You know as well as I that magic is not an art that allows for more than one star at any given time. 

#### CHARLIE  

It was my show, Fogg. One that you agreed to participate in, I might remind you. 

#### JACK  

I made a bet 

#### CHARLIE 

A bad bet. 

#### JACK 

Yes, a bad bet. You won, the fair wasn’t in New York and -

#### CHARLIE

And you had to be my apprentice for the fair. Those were the terms. 

#### JACK

I held up my end of the bargain. 

#### CHARLIE 

_[a short laugh]_ You most certainly did not. And you speak as if there was an end of the bargain that was mine, it wasn’t a deal, it was a bet, one that you lost.

#### JACK 

With dignity and much sportsmanship -

#### CHARLIE 

You barely acted as an apprentice, as evidenced by your insistence that we perform one of your perilous escapology routines -

#### JACK  

You were as eager to do it as I was. What greater example of your ability to control an audience than distracting them from a man in mortal danger. 

#### CHARLIE  

You’re fortunate I wasn’t distracted from your feats otherwise you’d very likely be dead. 

#### JACK 

I’m curious, when people ask about the illusion, what do you tell them? How do you describe what took place? 

#### CHARLIE 

You assume people ask about it. 

#### JACK

They didn’t hear of it in Europe? It’s all anyone talked to me about for years. 

#### CHARLIE 

People were more interested in the illusions I was currently performing for them, not past acts they’d heard about through rumor and hearsay. 

#### JACK 

And lord knows, Charles Chambers would never stoop to the art of escapologists again. 

#### CHARLIE 

It didn’t go particularly well the first time. Are you telling me that you’ve continued to perform such tricks? 

#### JACK

Of course I have. I had to prove that I could, didn’t I? 

#### CHARLIE 

How do you describe it to people when they ask? I’m sure I’m the villain of the whole piece. 

#### JACK 

Every story needs a villain. 

#### CHARLIE 

And a hero. Which I imagine you slot yourself into quite nicely. 

#### JACK 

Of course. I’m the brave and beleaguered magician who is always overlooked in favor of the classic but less substantive illusionist. When I’m given the opportunity to perform at the great Columbian Exposition, I can hardly say no. It could be the moment I’ve been waiting for, when the public finally starts to see me as the incredible artist I am.

#### CHARLIE 

_[playing along]_ But you’re foiled by the less substantive magician. 

#### JACK 

Not before he presents himself as an ally. You see, this magician considered me something of a mentee, what with my considerably younger age 

#### CHARLIE  

You are exactly five weeks younger than me 

#### JACK 

And in his encouraging tutelage, he gives me the honor of choosing what our closing illusion will be. 

#### CHARLIE 

More like I was browbeaten into it 

#### JACK 

And he had already been planning a trick of electricity-a bit of a fad, if you ask me, not truly inventive 

#### CHARLIE 

You seemed plenty excited about it when you discovered you could make it even more dangerous 

#### JACK 

But this magician was certain the audience would be wowed by lightning coming out of his finger tips. 

#### CHARLIE 

They would’ve been 

#### JACK 

But not nearly as awed as if that electricity were being sent into a pool of water and that pool of water was beneath a man in chains, and that man in chains was being slowly lowered toward the pool and his certain death should he be unable to escape. That is truly inventive. 

#### CHARLIE 

Inventive and needlessly treacherous. 

#### JACK 

This is what I proposed to the older magician 

#### CHARLIE 

_[under his breath]_ Five weeks. 

#### JACK 

And this was decided to be the grand finale of our act. A bombastic, thrillingly dangerous duet of feats. So intricate and bold the audience, though they know they are being fooled, will never be able to deduce exactly how. 

#### CHARLIE  

That’s because too much of it wasn’t an illusion. It’s easy to mask the architecture of an illusion when the feats you’re performing are real. 

#### JACK 

That’s exactly what makes it a great trick.

#### CHARLIE  

That’s exactly what makes it not a trick at all. 

#### JACK

But the audience doesn’t know that. All the audience knows is that they’re watching two great illusionists perform something they’ve never seen before. Something they could have never conceived of. 

#### CHARLIE 

You think I’m a great illusionist? 

#### JACK 

What? _[then]_ That’s not- I’m not done with my story, Mister Chambers. 

#### CHARLIE 

_[dry]_ By all means, continue. 

#### JACK 

And the death-defying stunt went off without a hitch when the magicians performed it in rehearsals and then soared to new heights when they began their public displays. It was all that anyone could talk about from the fair, each performance more amazing than the last for the number of times it had already been performed with perfection. Even the older, stuffy, stuck-in-his-ways 

#### CHARLIE 

You were just complimenting me a moment ago 

#### JACK

Even he seemed galvanized by the project. And then, a fateful day in September arrived, the stage was set as it always was, the fresh audience enraptured throughout the entire performance, the exactitude of the older magician’s art elevated by the imagination of the younger. 

#### CHARLIE

_[a laugh]_“Elevated”. 

#### JACK 

Yes, elevated. It felt as if the whole theater was elevated, floating up into the heavens, lifted by the gasps of the crowd, propelled by their collective wonder. _[then]_ The whole room held their breath as the older magician explained what was about to take place. As the brave, young illusionist was wrapped in chains and tied to the rope that would lower him toward the water, nervous titters went through the audience, horrified murmurings as they all wondered if they were about to watch a man die. 

#### CHARLIE 

And did they? 

#### JACK 

A simple question with a very complicated answer. The illusion began, the lightning shooting out from the magician’s palms and into the pool of water as his apprentice struggled against his restraints, getting lower and lower, the electricity crackling over the surface of the water that was mere feet below him. It was then that both magicians started to sense that something was wrong. The chains were not coming loose in the way they were supposed to, the bound man being lowered far too quickly, the electricity more powerful than it ever had been. _[beat]_ That is when the older magician started to panic. And nothing can ruin an illusion like panic. 

#### CHARLIE  

Seems to me he had a good reason for panicking. 

#### JACK  

The escapologist would have gotten free. He just needed another moment. 

#### CHARLIE 

He didn’t have another moment 

#### JACK 

_[talking over him]_ But the senior decided for them both, rushing to stop the electricity and shattering the illusion. 

#### CHARLIE 

Despite what you may think of my ability to control an audience’s attention, I don’t think anyone was paying me any mind at that point, not with you still chained up and hurtling toward certain death. 

#### JACK 

No, they weren’t paying attention to you at all, which meant that I’m the one who seemed incompetent. 

#### CHARLIE 

How-- 

#### JACK

Hush. I’m almost finished. _[then]_ The illusion was shattered and while there are plenty of magic enjoyers who like to learn the trick behind the trick, that was not the intent here. And even though the magician’s command of lightning stopped, he did it so swiftly and so adeptly, no one registered that he was flipping off a mechanical switch, not ceasing a spell. If anything, the abrupt end to the Hands of Thor made them seem that much more real, as if the magician truly did hold lightning in his hands. _[beat]_ And then suddenly the chains were real too. The people who had paid for a ticket to be fooled with skill were all at once uncertain they were being fooled at all-- 

#### CHARLIE 

Which was exactly the point I was trying to make -

#### JACK

And as the apprentice, who had failed to free himself, hit the water, he appeared by all accounts to be so incompetent as to die by his own trick. When the older magician rushed forward and dragged his unconscious body from the pool, he became the hero of the story, bravely saving the man who couldn’t keep up with his trick. Because the apprentice was alive-jolted, yes, but alive. Alive and of sound mind and body, sound enough to know when his equipment had been tampered with. 

#### CHARLIE

What?

#### JACK

That’s the end of it. I always think it’s good to leave people in suspense. Encourage them to hound me with questions that I will be sufficiently, intriguingly vague about. 

#### CHARLIE  

No, I- your equipment wasn’t tampered with, one of the chain links got 

#### JACK 

Got bent, I know, I remember the excuses. That’s as clear a sign of tampering as you can get. 

#### CHARLIE 

Or a clear sign that the manager of the theatre and his goons don’t know how to be delicate with other peoples’ things. And who exactly would have been tampering with the trick anyway? 

##### [A beat of silence]

#### CHARLIE 

You can’t be serious. Me? 

#### JACK 

Who had better reason and easier opportunities than you? 

#### CHARLIE 

Opportunities I’ll grant you, there’s no denying that, but what earthly reason would I have? 

#### JACK 

Ensure your position as the top illusionist in America - and now Europe as seems. Crowd the competition out of the field. 

#### CHARLIE  

What you’re accusing me of is not the sabotage of a competitor. It’s tantamount to attempted murder. 

#### JACK 

Mm. It is, isn’t it? 

#### CHARLIE 

John, you can’t really think 

#### JACK 

I don’t know what to think. All I know is that I woke up feeling as though I’d stuck my fingers directly into Tesla’s contraption and I no longer had the respect of anyone in our industry. 

#### CHARLIE 

I don’t think it was the hapless fumbling you remember it being. It was...the audience was frightened. There was no room for judgment of the performance, not when it was unclear whether you were alive. _[then]_ You tell the story as if it were a thrilling mystery novel, but that wasn’t the truth of it at all. 

#### JACK 

And how would you tell it, I wonder?

#### CHARLIE  

Well, if I were pressed to talk about it, I suppose I’d say that we were endeavoring to do a risky trick and something went wrong- they sometimes do in our business and while never pleasant, these mishaps rarely have dire consequences. But in this instance, given the already precarious circumstances of the illusion, the dangers of error or failure were much higher. I’d say we were both lucky to have walked away relatively unscathed. 

#### JACK 

Not quite as exciting as my retelling, is it? 

#### CHARLIE

I suppose not. 

##### [Beat]

#### CHARLIE 

I might also say that it was the most terrified I’d ever been in a performance. I used to get horrible stage anxiety, right before starting a show. My hands would tingle and go numb, my stomach would churn. It always seemed as if my heart were beating far faster than it was supposed to. As I did more and more shows, got better and better at my craft, those feelings went away. The stage became a domain in which I felt I had control. _[then]_ And then the World’s Fair. You build suspense well in your storytelling, Mister Fogg, but in my memory there was no suspense at all. It all happened too quickly for there to be anticipation of disaster. As many times as I’ve replayed those moments in my mind, I’m never able to recall with perfect clarity. It went by too quickly for me to register the details. I certainly didn’t switch off the Tesla coil in time for it to seem studied or in time to save you. There was no calculated choice, just hurried instinct. The only thing I truly remember is being scared. 

#### JACK 

I wasn’t scared. _[beat]_ Well, I suppose that’s not entirely correct. I wasn’t scared of the real danger, I was scared of not being able to escape, of ruining the trick. 

#### CHARLIE 

You care so little for your own life? 

#### JACK 

That isn’t it at all - I care a great deal. I value my life more than anything, and I have much more of it to live. But I don’t know that I thought I’d really die if I hit that water._[then]_ Even with all your worrying as we were building and then performing the illusion night after night, I never took it seriously. 

#### CHARLIE 

Yes, I know. 

#### JACK  

I thought you were being over-dramatic. 

#### CHARLIE 

I was not.

#### JACK 

Yes. I know. 

#### CHARLIE

I spoke to a friend of mine a few weeks after, a doctor I know. And he said you almost certainly would have died if I had not grabbed your arm the moment I did. 

#### JACK 

You pulled me from the jaws of death, is that it? 

#### CHARLIE  

More that I put my own body in between its teeth. The electrical current. It went through me as well and in doing so, left your body before it could do the damage it was capable of. 

#### JACK

Are you asking for my gratitude? 

#### CHARLIE

No, I’m not so naive as to believe I’m going to receive that. _[beat]_ But I do think you owe me the courtesy of not accusing me of attempted murder when I put my own life at risk to save yours. 

#### JACK  

Making yourself a gallant hero in the process. 

#### CHARLIE 

You have to be one of the most cynical men I’ve ever met. Which is quite the feat, considering you’re also the most prone to flights of fancy. 

#### JACK 

I’m teasing. Mostly. I don’t actually think you were trying to kill me in a dramatic public act. It wouldn’t be your style. If you were to get rid of competition, you wouldn’t do it on stage. I imagine you’d invite the man over for a drink and then poison his scotch. 

#### CHARLIE 

You’re probably right. Whereas you would most certainly make a spectacle of murder if you ever chose to undertake it. 

#### JACK 

I imagine I would. 

#### CHARLIE 

I suppose there’s victory in getting you to admit I didn’t intentionally sabotage you, but I’m not sure I can count it as such if you’re still angry with me. 

#### JACK 

Who said I’m angry with you? 

#### CHARLIE  

You did. Or as good as, considering all the grievances you’ve slung at me. Or is it just the late hour and the drink in your hand that drove those words?

#### JACK 

It is the late hour and the drink in my hand that allows me to admit I’m angry with myself, not you, and that I’m mostly resentful of your success. 

#### CHARLIE 

An honest drunk, then. How refreshing. 

#### JACK 

_[dry]_ And you appear to be a stoic drunk, which is so very different from your usual demeanor. 

#### CHARLIE

I’m not drunk at all, I haven’t had the opportunity really. I only got here just before midnight. 

#### JACK 

Other fabulous parties to show your face at I suppose? 

#### CHARLIE 

The opposite, in fact. I spent most of the evening in my room at the Astor, staring at the door in apprehension and ultimately deciding that I didn’t want to hear the clock chime twelve alone. 

#### JACK

So you lowered your standards to come to Hayward’s. 

#### CHARLIE 

It was hardly that. This was the only invitation I received. But I’ve been gone for so long, haven’t seen many of these people in years and I wasn’t sure...Well, there's a reason I came out on the balcony looking for some peace and quiet. 

#### JACK 

I’m sorry you didn’t find it. 

#### CHARLIE 

I’m not. Despite a vague desire to reintegrate myself into New York society, I’m not sure I have the first idea about how to socialize with these people. 

#### JACK  

But you do know how to socialize with me? 

#### CHARLIE 

I believe I do. Or, at the very least, I don’t worry about it so much. 

#### JACK 

_[sarcastic]_ I’m flattered that you put so much thought into our interactions. 

#### CHARLIE 

Oh, come now, I don’t mean it like that. I know you better than most here, largely because of the period we worked together, but I also know you have an enormous amount of disdain for me. Or, as you put it, resentment. 

#### JACK 

And that makes conversation easier does it? 

#### CHARLIE 

It makes it simpler, in some ways, yes. So many of these people speak in riddles; the delicate obsfucation of polite society. You rarely hide what you think of me, Mister Fogg. As I said  refreshing.

For someone who doesn’t like prevaricating, you’re awfully good at it. I may speak plainly with you, but I’m not certain you return the favor.

No? Maybe I’m not as immediately forthcoming, but you can ask me anything. I’ll answer. 

#### JACK 

How have you been, really, since ninety-three. 

#### CHARLIE  

I’m not sure what you mean. 

#### JACK 

See, there you go, evading 

#### CHARLIE 

I’m not evading, I’m genuinely perplexed. I told you how I’ve been - I’ve been in Europe mostly, enjoying a degree of success, traveling to beautiful places - and now I’m back and not particularly skilled at resettling in the life I have here. If this is about what you asked earlier, if I ever thought of you and what your life might be like, the truth of the matter is I did. I worried for you, that you would continue to try dangerous stunts, and kill yourself more completely in an act so I followed the news of your career as well as I could. 

#### JACK 

Not that there was much news to follow. 

#### CHARLIE 

You may not have had the kind of meteoric rise that you were hoping for, but as far as I can tell, you’ve made a decent trade in the smaller theaters. And when that Houdini fellow came along and started doing his escapes, you pivoted well. You’re very good at adapting to circumstance, to filling the areas of magic that are otherwise neglected. 

#### JACK 

And being relegated to legerdemain and base levitation is exactly what I hoped to be doing. 

#### CHARLIE 

There is always going to be a desire for sleight of hand. Dexterity never fails to astound when performed well and I’m sure you perform it well. So yes, I did think of your career, and by my estimation, you’ve been doing just fine, as well as, if not better, than you were doing before the fair. Smaller scale illusions and smaller theaters maybe, but there’s nothing wrong with that. 

#### JACK 

Easy for the man playing the largest venues in the world to say.

#### CHARLIE 

Does that answer your question satisfactorily? 

#### JACK 

It answers a question, but you still haven’t told me how you truly have been feeling. 

#### CHARLIE  

You ask the question as if there’s some deeper significance I’m meant to know.

Because there is. I wasn’t certain there would be, but when I saw you across the room earlier, in the light of the parlor, I realized... 

#### CHARLIE 

You realized what? 

#### JACK 

Do you really not see it? 

#### CHARLIE 

I don’t know what I’m meant to see. 

##### [Jack steps forward, bringing himself closer to Charlie]

#### JACK  

Look at me. Really look. Examine me closely. 

#### CHARLIE 

_[nervous]_ Why would I do that? 

#### JACK  

Don’t you see what I see? Don’t you recognize the phenomenon that’s been staring back at you from the mirror every day for ten years?

What? 

#### JACK

#### CHARLIE  

I’m not sure I know what you’re saying. 

#### JACK 

Look at me! I may have my moments of vanity but it isn’t vanity that looks at my reflection - the reflection of a man nearing forty - and sees a spry twenty-nine year old looking back.

You do look well.

#### CHARLIE

You think..._[laughing]_ You think that our magic trick...what, made us young?

I don’t know. All I know is that I’ve felt different since that day and each year I expect to look different and...I don’t. You don’t. 

##### [Charles starts to pace away]

#### CHARLIE 

An honest drunk and a delusional one. 

#### JACK

#### CHARLIE 

No, I’m not delusional, and I’m also not inebriated enough for this conversation. So I think it’s best we end it. 

##### [He walks to the balcony door]

#### JACK 

Charles-- 

##### [He stops]

#### CHARLIE 

I’m sorry the trick went wrong, John. I truly am. But you need to move on. 

##### [He opens the door, the hubbub from inside bleeding out]

Happy New Year, Mister Fogg. 

##### [He walks inside, closing the door]

#### JACK 

_[muttering]_ Happy damn New Year.
