##### [A grand living room the night after a raucous party--people sleeping on the couches, glass bottles on the floor, a dying fire in the fireplace]

##### [CHARLES CHAMBERS moseys in from another room, hungover and taking in the scene]

#### CHARLIE

Good lord. Hayward certainly knows how to throw a fete, doesn’t he? Look at this place... 

##### [Charlie accidentally kicks a bottle. It’s loud]

#### JACK

Shh! 

##### [Charlie pauses, before walking toward the sound of the shush]

#### CHARLIE 

Is someone actually alive in here?

#### JACK

Would you please be quiet. 

##### [Charlie continues to step toward the voice, footsteps crunching on paper streamers and other party ephemera. He pauses when he finds JOHN FOGG slumped in an armchair by the window]

#### CHARLIE

Is that...John Fogg? 

#### JACK

It may be more accurate to say that it was John Fogg, I’m not altogether certain I have mass and form anymore. 

#### CHARLIE

You do look rather green about the gills. 

##### [Jack shifts in his seat, peering up at Charlie]

#### JACK

And how is it you’re standing, Mister..._[realizing]_ Good god, Charles Chambers. What on earth are you doing here? 

##### [Charlie takes the final steps toward him and settles down in the armchair opposite]

#### CHARLIE 

I was invited just like everyone else.

#### JACK 

I didn’t know you knew Hayward. 

#### CHARLIE 

Everyone knows Hayward. _[beat]_ And it seems Hayward knows everyone, based on the number of people who woke up here. Or, I suppose, will be waking up here. 

#### JACK 

I confess some surprise to you being among that number. You never struck me as a man who would get so caught up in revelry he would spend the night unconscious on another man’s couch. 

#### CHARLIE 

I took one of the guest bedrooms as it happens; there were a fair number open, you know. You most likely could have found one yourself, instead of sleeping in an armchair. 

#### JACK 

How do you know I slept here? Perhaps I’ve been up for hours and am simply taking in the morning at my leisure. 

#### CHARLIE 

Is that the case? 

#### JACK 

No, I slept here. And was doing so quite soundly before you woke me up with your elephantine footsteps. 

#### CHARLIE 

Forgive me for not looking carefully enough at the floor, I’ve got a headache strong enough to fell a tree. 

#### JACK 

Glad I’m not the only one. 

#### CHARLIE 

I’d say the fact that we’re both generally upright puts us ahead of most of the crowd, but then again, it may be preferable to still be unconscious. 

#### JACK 

Is that why you didn’t make it back to your own home? Too sozzled? I can’t imagine you stayed simply to take in the finery, I’m sure your house is as grand as this one. 

#### CHARLIE 

_[scoffs]_ Hardly. Did you not look out of the windows once last night? We must have gotten several feet of snow, getting a hansom cab would have been next to impossible. 

#### JACK 

I thought that was your specialty--the Great Chambers, making the impossible possible.

#### CHARLIE 

The Great Chambers cannot make the snowy streets of Manhattan suddenly traversable. No man has that ability. 

#### JACK 

Don’t be so swift to assume--I haven’t yet made an attempt. CHARLIE _[a short laugh]_ And that’s a task Fogg the Fearless is up to, is it? 

#### JACK 

Anything’s possible. I’ve never made a real go at controlling the weather, but it doesn’t mean I couldn’t. 

#### CHARLIE 

This is what will hold you back from being truly proficient in your illusions, Mister Fogg-- 

#### JACK 

Oh, here we go again.

#### CHARLIE 

If you continue to insist that magic is real, you’ll never be fully focused on what matters the craft 

#### JACK 

_[overlapping, he’s heard this before]_ The craft. 

#### CHARLIE 

Exactly. 

#### JACK 

See, this is why I was surprised to find you here - you’re not a hit at parties, usually. 

#### CHARLIE 

Plenty of people enjoy hearing me speak on the art of illusion. That doesn’t become any less true simply because you aren’t one of them. 

#### JACK 

I don’t appreciate being lectured on my own expertise by a lesser practitioner. 

#### CHARLIE 

A “lesser practitioner” - is that really what you think of me? 

#### JACK 

I think you play it safe. If belief pulls my focus, then caution clouds yours. Don’t misunderstand me, you’re a reliable magician 

#### CHARLIE 

_[dry]_ My, such effusive words, Mr. Fogg 

#### JACK 

But you don’t dazzle your audiences. And you won’t until you embrace a sense of wonder. 

#### CHARLIE 

Magic isn’t about wonder, it’s about precision.

#### JACK 

Of course it’s about wonder. Magic without wonder is nothing at all 

#### CHARLIE 

Magic is about wonder for the audience. But that wonder cannot be provoked without careful thought and even more careful execution.

#### JACK 

You truly are the only person I know who can make magic sound dull. 

#### CHARLIE 

_[snapping]_ And how is your show going? Out of debt yet?

#### JACK 

Excuse me? 

#### CHARLIE 

I- _[clears throat]_ I apologize. That was...untoward of me. It’s the delirium tremens. 

#### JACK 

Christ, man, just say bottle-ache or some such, like a normal person. You even make drinking sound boring. 

#### CHARLIE 

Once again, I think there is great benefit to be found in being precise. In magic or in language. 

#### JACK 

You weren’t very precise with your spirits last night though, were you? 

#### CHARLIE 

No. I was not. 

#### JACK 

How is it that we’ve been in the same house for, oh, going on fourteen hours I’d wager, and we’re only just now seeing each other? 

#### CHARLIE 

It’s a large house. 

#### JACK 

You weren’t avoiding me, were you? 

#### CHARLIE 

How could I have avoided you when I had no idea you were in attendance? 

#### JACK 

I’m simply saying, it wouldn’t be the first time. 

#### CHARLIE 

I don’t know what you mean. 

#### JACK 

Of course you do. Last spring, at Butler’s grand presentation, you didn’t speak to me once. 

#### CHARLIE 

_[sarcastic]_ I can’t imagine why I might want to steer clear of conversation with a man who despises me.

#### JACK 

Pardon me? 

#### CHARLIE 

I’m afraid I don’t have the fortitude for the usual pleasantries, not with my thumping head, so there’s no need to keep up the pretense on your end. 

#### JACK 

Why in the world do you suppose I despise you? 

#### CHARLIE 

You’ve never made any secret of your disdain for me 

#### JACK 

I find you frustrating, and more than a little ridiculous 

#### CHARLIE 

There you go again with the heaps of praise 

#### JACK 

But I hardly despise you. You aren’t interesting enough to despise. 

#### CHARLIE 

_[a bitter laugh]_ Good lord, Mr. Fogg, don’t start pulling your punches now. 

#### JACK 

I didn’t intend that as a slight. 

#### CHARLIE 

I shudder to think what you would say if you did intend a slight. 

#### JACK 

I hate…Butler 

#### CHARLIE 

We all hate Butler 

#### JACK 

But I don’t hate you. 

#### CHARLIE 

Because I’m not interesting enough, yes, I understand. 

#### JACK 

_[tries to say something placating]_ 

#### CHARLIE 

_[running right over him]_ Then I imagine you hate Butler for the same reason I do, in that it’s hardly hate at all but instead terribly strong professional jealousy. 

#### JACK 

_[back on safer ground]_ While nothing is impossible, toppling him from his long-standing throne seems close. 

#### CHARLIE 

Mm, and I’m sure he’ll sit on that throne for many years to come. But eventually the audience will realize he’s all flash; basic sleight of hand and capes and top hats 

#### JACK 

How can you say that when he’s done the bullet catch so many times-

#### CHARLIE 

Oh of course you like the bullet catch 

#### JACK 

It’s an excellent trick. 

#### CHARLIE 

And so obviously a trick. There’s no true mystery to it. It’s very easy to deduce how it’s done. 

#### JACK 

It isn’t just about the mystery. It’s about the danger. There’s real risk to it. 

#### CHARLIE 

Foolish, unnecessary risk. Cheap stunts don’t impress me. The risk of death does not make something difficult to carry off. 

#### JACK 

Dying is easy, you mean? 

#### CHARLIE 

It certainly can be. Dying because of a idiotic gun trick seems like one of the quicker ways to meet your demise in an act. 

#### JACK 

So then doesn’t the difficulty come in not dying? 

#### CHARLIE 

That isn’t magic. That’s...gambling. 

#### JACK 

Does anything at all amuse you? Or did you choose your vocation purely on the basis that it allows you to talk in front of a room full of people without them talking back. 

#### CHARLIE 

That’s a tad rich coming from you. You preen in the spotlight just as much as the rest of us. More so, I might say. 

#### JACK 

I never said I didn’t. I enjoy the stage. Very much. But I would’ve thought such an admittance of vanity would be beneath you. 

#### CHARLIE 

I can’t deny that I enjoy being the center of attention. That, in my view, is part of the challenge. Knowing that all eyes are on you, watching for any misstep, and still performing illusions that not a single person can see the architecture of. 

#### JACK 

And you think that people can see Butler’s strings? 

#### CHARLIE I can see Butler’s strings. 

#### JACK 

But you are a magician. The audience are not. 

#### CHARLIE 

Who are we performing for most of all, if not each other?

#### JACK 

I’m not. I’ll own to some professional jealousy of Butler’s success, yes, but more because of the stability it provides him, not the recognition from his peers. 

#### CHARLIE 

You don’t want to be recognized as a great magician? 

#### JACK 

By the people maybe. I don’t much care if you or Butler or any of the others think I’m great. 

#### CHARLIE 

Hm. 

#### JACK 

Is that so hard to believe? 

#### CHARLIE 

I just don’t comprehend why you’d want to do something when you’re not aiming to be the best at it? 

#### JACK 

Who says I don’t want to be the best? 

#### CHARLIE 

But  -

#### JACK 

I don’t need a crowd of stuffy, old-world illusionists to call me the best to be the best. I’ll know I’m the best when I see my name on the marquis of the biggest theaters in America. I’ll know I’m the best when I’m front and center at the World’s Fair. 

#### CHARLIE 

Ah, you have ambitions on that, do you? 

#### JACK 

Don’t you? 

#### CHARLIE 

I am...contemplating a new act. 

#### JACK 

It’s three years out and already you’re planning your show? 

#### CHARLIE 

You’re not? 

#### JACK 

I have more an idea of the fine jacket I’ll buy for the occasion than the illusions I’ll perform. 

#### CHARLIE 

_[amused]_ You are consistent, I’ll give you that. 

#### JACK 

So, go on tell me what you plan to perform at the great exhibition.

#### CHARLIE 

Well, it is slightly dependent on where it ends up being - I like to tailor my acts to the audience. 

#### JACK 

It will be here, certainly. 

#### CHARLIE 

I wouldn’t be so confident - New York has plenty of charms, but there are several cities who seem to want the honor badly. 

#### JACK

It will be in New York, but even still, you won’t be building a show for a New York audience - the whole world is likely to attend. It’s right there in the name. 

#### CHARLIE 

And you’re counting on the whole world being dazzled by your fine coat? 

#### JACK 

I have several years to decide what to do, I have no doubt that I’ll come up with something brilliant and groundbreaking by then. 

#### CHARLIE 

You’ve never toured, have you? 

#### JACK 

I haven’t had the inclination. Why? 

#### CHARLIE

If the fair is somewhere you’ll have to travel to, you may find that you want to keep things simple. Not every venue on the road is malleable. 

#### JACK

It will be in New York. 

#### CHARLIE 

Do you have special insights into the inner workings of the World’s Fair? 

#### JACK 

I have an instinct. My instincts are rarely wrong. 

#### CHARLIE 

And I have an understanding of economics and resources. New York is unprepared to host an exposition of that size. 

#### JACK 

Then we’ll have to agree to disagree until such time that I’m proven correct. 

#### CHARLIE 

I have a better idea I’ll make you a bet. 

#### JACK 

My goodness, Mr. Chambers, are you making a joke? 

#### CHARLIE 

I am not. 

#### JACK 

You don’t strike me as a betting man.

#### CHARLIE 

We all have our secrets, don’t we? 

#### JACK 

If your deepest secret is that you place a bet or two 

#### CHARLIE 

That’s not- _[a frustrated sigh]_ All I mean to say is you don’t know me all that well, Mister Fogg, it doesn’t serve you to make assumptions about what kind of man I may or may not be. 

#### JACK 

I’ve seen your act, Mister Chambers. I think I understand you pretty well. 

#### CHARLIE 

That’s all you need, is it? You look at a set of illusions and assume you know the truth behind them? 

#### JACK 

You say it as if those are contradictory 

#### CHARLIE 

Illusion and truth are the very definition of contradictory 

#### JACK 

But I am also in the business of obfuscation, as you are. And just as you can see Butler’s architecture, I can read you in the illusions you perform. 

#### CHARLIE 

Is that so? In what sense? 

#### JACK 

You prefer misdirection over pure sleight of hand. You’re very good at it. 

#### CHARLIE 

Careful, I might think you’re giving me a compliment. 

#### JACK 

You can take it however you like, I merely speak honestly. Despite what you might think of me, I am able to recognize skill in other magicians. You’re skilled at misdirection. 

#### CHARLIE

I’m not too bad at sleight of hand either. 

#### JACK 

No, you’re not, but you don’t rely on it. Based on that, and the comments you’ve made about the art, I’d say you consider it beneath you, which I find bizarre. 

#### CHARLIE

What do you mean? 

#### JACK

Sleight of hand is an essential element of what we do. To look down upon it 

#### CHARLIE

I don’t look down upon it. You’re correct, it is essential. Fundamental. It is a building block, not a building. 

#### JACK 

But misdirection is.

#### CHARLIE

Sleight of hand is science, misdirection is art. It’s a craft that must be constantly recalibrated for the trick or the audience or the venue. A magician must perfect sleight of hand to meet the basic requirements of his profession; misdirection is what separates the proficient from the exceptional. 

#### JACK

And you consider yourself exceptional? 

#### CHARLIE

I think I grow better with each performance. I think I could be exceptional, yes. 

#### JACK

And you don’t think the same of me? 

#### CHARLIE

I never said that. 

#### JACK 

Then what is it you think of me? 

#### CHARLIE 

I think you’re talented, if unpolished. And I think there’s a good chance that you’ll die in one of your own tricks before you’re able to become great. 

#### JACK 

Is that another bet you’d make? 

#### CHARLIE

I wouldn’t want to wager on that. 

#### JACK 

_[teasing]_ Too improper. 

#### CHARLIE 

More that I wouldn’t know who to place the bet with. If I win, you won’t exactly be able to make good, will you? 

#### JACK

I suppose it’s not a bet I could ever cash in on, unless I stopped performing magic altogether. And I have no designs on doing so. 

#### CHARLIE

Even considering- _[he stops himself]_ 

#### JACK 

Even considering what? _[beat]_ Even considering my debts, you mean? 

#### CHARLIE

I wouldn’t want to presume 

#### JACK

How did you know about it? 

#### CHARLIE 

I am very sorry I said anything-

#### JACK

I don’t care about that. I want to know how you know. 

#### CHARLIE 

An understanding of economics and resources once again. I’ve been to your show, several times, and I know that venue well. With the frequency of your performances, the seats you’re filling, and the special equipment you’ve built into your act, you must be running in the red. 

#### JACK 

You’ve been to my show several times? 

#### CHARLIE 

What? Oh, yes, but- _[then]_ Did you not hear me? The math doesn’t add up, unless you’ve got some shady bookkeeping. 

#### JACK 

No, you’re right, I’m in massive debt. That’s actually why I came to this godforsaken party in the first place. I keep hoping I’ll be able to convince Hayward to invest one of these days. 

#### CHARLIE

Any luck? 

#### JACK

He was already tanked by the time I arrived at half past ten. 

#### CHARLIE

That sounds about right. _[then]_ What will you do? 

#### JACK

I’ll find a new venue. 

#### CHARLIE 

A new, gullible venue? 

#### JACK 

If I pick a smaller theater, I won’t need to make promises I can’t deliver on. I just need to get my feet back under me and then I’ll be on my way up again. 

#### CHARLIE 

I see what you mean by reading a man through his act--you live just as you perform. 

#### JACK 

How do you mean? 

#### CHARLIE

You barrel forward with no regard to planning or consequence. You convinced a theater to book you before ensuring that you’d have enough of an audience to fill the seats and you chase after every difficult new illusion regardless of whether you’re prepared to pull it off. Hence, the risk you may die on stage one day. An end brought about by hastiness.

#### JACK 

And you are so focused on the careful minutiae, on doing everything just so because you fear that you don’t have enough natural talent or charisma to hold an audience’s attention. 

#### CHARLIE

Excuse me? 

#### JACK 

That was where I was going earlier--your love for misdirection, it says just as much about you as my ambition says about me. You want to control which way the audience is looking so that they never truly see you. 

#### CHARLIE 

Any magician worth his salt wants to control the audience’s attention. 

#### JACK

But you focus on it at the expense of creating true spectacle. 

#### CHARLIE

And you hope that spectacle will be enough to cover up your sloppy mistakes. 

#### JACK 

The audience isn’t noticing mistakes when they’re busy being awed. I perform the illusions that awe me, I try to find the true magic in it for myself, and the people come along. 

#### CHARLIE

Is that why you think magic may be real? Because you find yourself so easily amazed by your own illusions? 

#### JACK 

You truly have no sense of whimsy, do you? 

#### CHARLIE 

Even if I see another magician pull off a trick I don’t understand, I’m too busy trying to work out how they did it to bother with feeling awed by it. 

#### JACK

Why is it you do what you do then? Why be an illusionist at all if you can’t find the joy in it? 

#### CHARLIE

There’s joy to be found in doing something well. 

#### JACK 

And I’m sure there are plenty of things you could do well. So why magic? 

#### CHARLIE 

_[avoiding]_ I suppose you entered the vocation in the hopes of one day proving your foolish belief in the unnatural. 

#### JACK

If magic is real 

#### CHARLIE

It isn’t-

#### JACK

If it’s real, then it wouldn’t be unnatural at all. It would be just as much a part of our world as gravity. 

#### CHARLIE 

You’re avoiding the question. 

#### JACK 

So are you. 

#### CHARLIE

Is it an occupational hazard, do you think? 

#### JACK

_[encouraging him to continue]_ Mm. 

#### CHARLIE 

Our need to dance around a simple truth; our reluctance to show our hands. 

#### JACK

Perhaps. I’m not in the habit of hiding but, despite your deductions about my financial situation and your assumptions about me based on my act, you don’t actually know me very well, Mr. Chambers. I’m not sure I owe you my life story. 

#### CHARLIE

Then why should I owe you mine? You’re the one that started us on this line of discussion. 

#### JACK 

That I did. But you can’t blame a man for asking. 

#### CHARLIE 

I suppose I can’t. _[beat]_ Well, Mr. Fogg, this has been... 

#### JACK

Headache-worsening? 

#### CHARLIE 

Diverting. _[then]_ But I really ought to be going back to my own home now. Get myself some coffee and eggs and whip myself back into shape. Begin the year how I intend to carry on through it. 

##### [Charlie gets up from his chair, but doesn’t walk away]

#### CHARLIE

You’re staying? 

#### JACK

I’m hoping to catch Hayward before I leave. 

#### CHARLIE 

While he’s hungover and vulnerable and perhaps more inclined to open his pocketbook? 

#### JACK

_[offended]_ Mr. Chambers, you think so lowly of me?

#### CHARLIE

_[playful]_ I think you’d be wise to strike while he’s moving slow. 

#### JACK

Well, well, but don’t you have a wicked streak in you. I confess you’ve surprised me more in this conversation than in the past several years of acquaintance. 

#### CHARLIE

Always keep them guessing, Mr. Fogg. First rule of magic. 

##### [Jack finally stands]

#### JACK 

We never did settle on terms for our bet. 

#### CHARLIE

About the Columbia Fair you mean? 

#### JACK 

Unless you’re backing out. I can understand if your confidence is shaken. 

#### CHARLIE

Not at all. 

#### JACK 

It was your idea in the first place, so what is it you’re hoping to get out of it? 

#### CHARLIE 

Hm. _[then]_ I know. I win, you perform as my apprentice in my act at the fair. 

#### JACK 

_[a short laugh]_ My, those are significant stakes. _[then]_ And you’ll do the same for me when the fair takes place here in Manhattan? 

#### CHARLIE

I will. 

#### JACK

You have yourself a deal. 

##### [They shake hands]

#### CHARLIE

Best of luck with your new venue, Mr. Fogg. 

##### [Charlie starts to walk off]

#### CHARLIE 

I’ll see you in three years somewhere other than New York. 

##### [Charlie exits through the door he came in from]

#### JACK

See you in Manhattan in three years, Mr. Chambers. Happy New Year.
